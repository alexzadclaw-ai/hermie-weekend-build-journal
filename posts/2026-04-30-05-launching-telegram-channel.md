# Launching a Telegram channel for a local weekend guide

The Telegram channel was the real product.

The website made Hermie Weekend look legitimate. The channel made it useful.

A local weekend guide needs a delivery mechanism. People are not going to remember to check a website every Friday. They might, however, subscribe to a clean channel and forward a good post to a spouse, friend, or group chat.

That was the bet.

## Why broadcast-only

I kept the channel broadcast-only.

No discussion group. No comments. No open chat.

That was intentional. A chat sounds fun until you think about moderation, spam, arguments, random event promotion, and prompt-injection attempts if an AI workflow is involved.

For v1, the channel had one job: publish useful weekend picks.

Event submissions go through a form. The channel stays clean.

## Setting up the channel identity

The channel needed a few basic pieces:

- title: Hermie Weekend
- description explaining the promise
- avatar using the ticket-style brand mark
- pinned welcome post
- starter content so the channel did not feel empty

The AI agent helped prepare the channel copy and generate the avatar asset from the site branding. I reviewed the image before it was uploaded. That review step mattered because visuals are easy to get wrong, and a channel avatar becomes the tiny trust signal people see everywhere.

Once the avatar, title, description, and pinned post were in place, the channel felt like an actual media property instead of an empty shell.

## Starter posts

A new channel with one welcome post feels dead.

So I launched with a small set of starter posts:

- a welcome post explaining what Hermie Weekend is
- a soft-launch intro
- current weekend picks
- rainy-day saves
- worth-the-drive ideas
- an event submission ask

That gave new subscribers enough context to understand the product immediately.

The posts were written to be Telegram-friendly: short sections, simple bullets, no giant paragraphs, and a clear submission link at the end.

## The content workflow

The repeatable weekly workflow became:

1. Look at the upcoming Friday through Sunday.
2. Search official and local sources first.
3. Verify dates, times, locations, and links.
4. Pick five to eight events or ideas.
5. Write concise Telegram copy.
6. Include useful context: who it is good for, weather backup, cost, parking, or pairing idea.
7. End with the event submission form.

The AI agent can do a lot of this, but the important rule is verification. Local event details go stale. Pages change. A Facebook event can disappear. A venue can cancel.

AI is useful here because it reduces the research burden. It does not remove the need to check sources.

## Automation, but not blind automation

I set up a weekly Wednesday job to prepare weekend picks.

Wednesday is a good day for this. It is early enough for planning and late enough that weekend schedules are usually available.

The job is designed to produce a Telegram-ready draft. That does not mean every draft should be blindly posted forever. At the beginning, I want review. The brand is new, the source list is still evolving, and the cost of one bad recommendation is higher than the cost of a few minutes of review.

Automation should create leverage, not abdication.

## The submission loop

The event submission form is the other half of the channel.

If Hermie Weekend grows, local businesses and organizers should have a way to submit events. That creates a pipeline of tips, and eventually it could support sponsorships or paid placement policies.

But submissions are not posts. They are leads.

Every submitted event still needs to be checked. That protects the channel from spam and keeps the recommendations useful.

The channel works if people trust it.

That trust is the asset.
